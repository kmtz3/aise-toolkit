# AISE Score Cards

Per-session scoring dimensions (0–5 each). Use when prepping to hit criteria or scoring a delivered session.

---

# ⚙️ Technical Discovery

## 1) Discovery upfront contract and session outcome framing (0–5)

**Ask a question**

"How completely did the AISE set the agenda and frame the session as a discovery workshop to design a successful onboarding program?"

**Instruction guide**

Definition. Must include all elements explicitly:

A) Time check.
B) Frame. This is a discovery workshop to understand current ways of working, not a feature training session.
C) Session outcome. What we want to clarify by the end (setup focus, process differences, gaps, big picture operating model).
D) Participation guidance. How the session will run (timers, topic flow, questions captured, collaboration expectations).
E) Next step logic. How this discovery will be used in onboarding design / configuration / follow-up sessions.

The Onboarding Deck Template explicitly frames the session this way, including timing, topic-by-topic sections, and desired outcomes.

**Scoring**

5 = A, B, C, D, E explicit and customer agrees.
4 = A, B, C, D, E explicit, customer agreement not explicit.
3 = Exactly 3 to 4 elements explicit.
2 = Exactly 2 elements explicit.
1 = Only 1 element explicit.
0 = None.

---

## 2) Organizational structure and stakeholder mapping discovery (0–5)

**Ask a question**

"Did the AISE uncover who will use Productboard, how the product organization is structured, and which teams/products should be represented in the setup?"

**Instruction guide**

Definition. To score high, the AISE must discover and document:

A) Product org structure (teams / tribes / squads).
B) Products or domains to be managed in Productboard.
C) Core roles involved (PMs, POs, design, engineering, other stakeholders).
D) Cross-functional stakeholders who influence process or will consume outputs.
E) Variations across teams (not just one team's process assumed as universal).

The deck explicitly asks for teams, product list, PM ownership, org diagram, and cross-functional roles.

**Scoring**

5 = Covers A, B, C, D, E with concrete examples and documents differences across teams.
4 = Covers A, B, C, D with concrete answers, limited detail on team differences.
3 = Covers at least 3 relevant areas but misses key stakeholder or product mapping detail.
2 = High-level org discussion only, little usable setup detail.
1 = Vague "who is here" conversation only.
0 = Not discussed.

## 3) Strategic alignment discovery (OKRs, goals, and connection to product work) (0–5)

**Ask a question**

"Did the AISE uncover how the customer defines and reviews strategic goals, and how those goals connect to product initiatives and delivery?"

**Instruction guide**

Definition. To score high, the AISE must cover:

A) Whether OKRs/goals exist at multiple levels (company, department, team).
B) Cadence for defining/reviewing goals.
C) Stakeholders responsible for each level.
D) How strategy maps to product work (objectives → initiatives → features, or equivalent).
E) Known disconnects or pain points between strategy and execution.

The deck includes explicit prompts and tables for OKR levels, cadence, responsible stakeholders, goal examples, and how goals connect to product work.

**Scoring**

5 = Covers A to E with concrete examples, including at least one real objective/KR or equivalent metric-based strategic target.
4 = Covers A to D with concrete answers, but disconnects/challenges not deeply explored.
3 = Basic strategy process discussed, but mapping to product execution remains vague.
2 = AISE asks generic strategy questions without capturing actionable structure.
1 = Only mentions "strategic alignment" at a high level.
0 = Not discussed.

## 8) Gap identification and setup implications synthesis (0–5)

**Ask a question**

"Did the AISE synthesize what they heard into setup priorities, team differences, and key gaps that will shape onboarding and configuration?"

**Instruction guide**

Definition. To score high, the AISE must explicitly summarize:

A) Priority areas for Productboard setup (e.g., feedback intake, hierarchy, prioritization fields, roadmap views).
B) Team/process differences that need to be accommodated or standardized.
C) Gaps/risks that may block success (ownership, unclear process, missing taxonomy, no governance, etc.).
D) Assumptions or open questions that need follow-up.
E) Confirmation from customer that the summary is accurate.

This aligns with the deck's stated session outcome to clarify setup focus, highlight process differences, surface gaps, and share the big-picture operating view.

**Scoring**

5 = A to E explicit, with customer confirmation and clear onboarding implications.
4 = A to D explicit, customer confirmation not explicit.
3 = AISE summarizes notes but not setup implications or risks clearly.
2 = Loose recap with no synthesis.
1 = No real summary, only "we got good info."
0 = Not present.

## 9) Close and next steps tied to discovery outputs (0–5)

**Ask a question**

"Did the AISE close with clear next steps, owners, and objectives tied to what was discovered in the session?"

**Instruction guide**

Definition. Must include:

A) Next step is explicit (e.g., configuration workshop, design decisions review, admin setup, integration planning, rollout/training planning).
B) Timing is explicit.
C) Required customer inputs or homework are assigned (owners + due dates where possible).
D) Objective for next session is tied to discovery findings/gaps from this session.
E) Customer agrees.

**Scoring**

5 = A, B, C, D, E explicit, with named owners and timing.
4 = A, B, D, E explicit, ownership/homework less specific.
3 = Next step agreed but objective generic or not tied to discovery outputs.
2 = AISE proposes follow-up but no commitment or clear objective.
1 = "We will follow up by email" only.
0 = No close.

# ⚡ Spark Workshop

## 1) Spark workshop framing and value outcome setting (0–5)

**Ask a question**

"How completely did the AISE frame the session as a Spark workflow onboarding workshop and set clear outcomes for what the customer should achieve during the session?"

**Instruction guide**

A) Time check and session flow.
B) Frame: this is a Spark onboarding workshop for real PM workflows, not just an AI feature demo.
C) Outcomes: align on target Spark use cases, establish context approach, run one or more practical workflows, and define next steps for adoption.
D) Participation expectations (PMs, Product Ops, PM leadership, and others as relevant).
E) Success criteria for the session (e.g., produce one usable artifact or complete one meaningful Spark job).

**Scoring**

5 = A, B, C, D, E explicit and customer alignment confirmed.
4 = A, B, C, D, E explicit, alignment not explicitly confirmed.
3 = Exactly 3 to 4 elements explicit.
2 = Exactly 2 elements explicit.
1 = Only 1 element explicit.
0 = None.

## 2) Spark use case prioritization and customer fit alignment (0–5)

**Ask a question**

"Did the AISE identify and prioritize the Spark use cases most relevant to the customer's PM pain points and maturity, instead of trying to cover everything?"

**Instruction guide**

Definition. To score high, the AISE must cover and capture:

A) Customer's top PM pain points relevant to Spark (feedback overload, PRD/brief creation, competitor research, context fragmentation, inconsistent PM artifacts).
B) Which Spark use cases are highest priority for this customer now (e.g., Feedback Analysis, Product Brief, Competitor Analysis).
C) Why those use cases are the best starting point (time to value, urgency, readiness, available inputs).
D) What is intentionally not covered today / deferred to later.
E) Alignment to the customer's team structure and PM process maturity.

Spark value is strongest when anchored to specific workflows, not broad "AI enablement."

**Scoring**

5 = Covers A to E with a clear, prioritized Spark use case focus.
4 = Covers A to D clearly, but process maturity/readiness fit is less explicit.
3 = Use cases are discussed, but prioritization or rationale is weak.
2 = AISE covers many Spark features without narrowing to customer priorities.
1 = Generic Spark overview only.
0 = Not covered.

## 3) Spark positioning and differentiation explained correctly (0–5)

**Ask a question**

"Did the AISE explain Spark's value and differentiation in a way that is accurate, practical, and relevant to the customer, especially versus generic AI tools?"

**Instruction guide**

Definition. To score high, the AISE must explain and connect:

A) Spark as a purpose-built PM workflow/productivity solution, not just AI chat.
B) Value of persistent/shared context and organizational intelligence.
C) Guided workflows / jobs as a way to improve quality and repeatability.
D) Why Spark can outperform generic AI for PM work when properly grounded in company context.
E) Realistic positioning (benefits plus current limitations/beta constraints).

Overhyping Spark kills credibility fast.

**Scoring**

5 = Covers A to E accurately, with customer-relevant examples.
4 = Covers A to D clearly, but limitations are not addressed strongly enough.
3 = Basic Spark value explained, but differentiation is generic or vague.
2 = Mostly AI hype or feature listing.
1 = Spark described superficially.
0 = Not covered.

## 4) Context strategy and shared context setup quality (0–5)

**Ask a question**

"Did the AISE help the customer define a practical context strategy for Spark, including what should be shared context versus session-specific inputs?"

**Instruction guide**

Definition. To score high, the AISE must cover and guide:

A) What types of information belong in shared/evergreen context (strategy, personas, product background, templates, recurring references).
B) What should remain use-case or chat-specific (initiative docs, current research, temporary inputs).
C) How to choose high-signal context sources first (not upload everything blindly).
D) Naming/organization standards for context assets.
E) How context quality affects Spark output quality and trust.

Poor context leads to generic output and failed adoption.

**Scoring**

5 = Covers A to E with a clear starter context plan and rationale.
4 = Covers A to D clearly, but output quality linkage is less explicit.
3 = Context is discussed, but strategy and organization standards remain vague.
2 = AISE shows context features without helping the customer define a plan.
1 = Only mentions "add context" at a high level.
0 = Not covered.

## 5) Main Spark workflow execution (Job or equivalent) on a real customer scenario (0–5)

**Ask a question**

"Did the AISE successfully guide the customer through at least one meaningful Spark workflow on a real customer scenario, not just a canned demo?"

**Instruction guide**

Definition. To score high, the AISE must:

A) Use a real customer scenario/problem/initiative where possible.
B) Run one core Spark workflow (e.g., Feedback Analysis, Product Brief, Competitor Analysis, or a comparable practical Spark task).
C) Involve customer participants in inputs/prompts/decisions during the workflow.
D) Produce a usable draft output/artifact (not just partial exploration).
E) Explain what made the result strong or weak (inputs, context, assumptions, missing data).

Workshop-style sessions should create a real "first value" moment.

**Scoring**

5 = Covers A to E with a real use case and a usable output produced.
4 = Covers A to D clearly, but quality analysis is less explicit.
3 = Workflow is run, but on a weak/canned scenario or with limited customer participation.
2 = AISE demos Spark output only, without collaborative execution.
1 = No meaningful workflow completed.
0 = Not covered.

1 = Minimal discussion of review quality.
0 = Not covered.
